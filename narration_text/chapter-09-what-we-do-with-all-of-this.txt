What We Do With All of This Seventy thousand years ago, a creature indistinguishable from you was drawing pictures in a cave in what is now France, carrying spears across a Eurasian steppe, and beginning to use language in a way that would, in the unfathomable future, produce everything described in this book. That creature had the same brain you have. The same emotional architecture, the same cognitive biases, the same capacity for both extraordinary kindness and extraordinary cruelty. What it lacked was accumulated knowledge ,  the layered understanding of reality that each generation left to the next, slowly building the cathedral of human understanding one stone at a time. You have inherited that cathedral. It is imperfect, unfinished, full of beautiful windows and load-bearing errors. Some of what passes for knowledge today will be recognized as superstition by the people who read their version of this book in two hundred years. This is not cause for despair ,  it is the definition of progress. We do not know what we don't know. The best we can do is maintain the honest curiosity and the institutional structures that allow errors to be corrected. Human knowledge is a collaborative, multigenerational conversation. You did not start this conversation and you will not end it. You are a node in a network that extends back to the first storyteller and forward to whoever comes after us ,  whoever they turn out to be. Your job is not to have the final word. It is to listen carefully, to contribute honestly, to correct what you find wrong, and to pass the conversation on in better shape than you received it. Yuval Noah Harari, in the book that functions as this volume's final chapter, asks what humanity will do with god-like powers it is in the process of acquiring: the ability to radically extend lifespan, to engineer happiness biochemically, to create intelligence that exceeds our own. The answer is not yet clear. It may be the most important question our species has ever faced. But here is what the long arc of human history suggests: we are, as a species, capable of more than we think. We built the institutions that limit tyranny. We abolished practices that seemed natural and inevitable. We cured diseases that once killed half of all children. We have been wrong about almost everything, repeatedly, and we have corrected ourselves, painfully and imperfectly, over and over. The reason to understand the mind is so you can think more clearly. The reason to understand science is so you can see reality more accurately. The reason to study history is so you can recognize patterns before they trap you. The reason to study philosophy is so you can act more wisely. The reason to understand society is so you can be less its unconscious product. The reason to understand technology is so you can be its author rather than its subject. And the reason to attend to the art of living is so that when your four thousand weeks are finished, you will have spent them on something that deserved them. This book ends here. The education continues until you do.  We are all just walking each other home. Ram Dass
