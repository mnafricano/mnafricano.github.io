Chapter 3: The Power of Stories We Tell Together Approximately seventy thousand years ago, something changed in the human brain. Archaeologists call it the Cognitive Revolution. What changed, exactly, remains debated ,  perhaps a mutation in the neural wiring for language ,  but the effect is visible in the fossil record: suddenly, humans were making art, burying their dead with ceremony, creating tools of remarkable sophistication, and, most importantly, cooperating in large groups with strangers. Other animals cooperate, but only with those they know personally and can monitor directly. A chimpanzee troop rarely exceeds 150 individuals, limited by the cognitive load of tracking social relationships. What allowed humans to build groups of thousands, then millions, then billions? Yuval Noah Harari, the historian who most clearly articulated this idea, has a striking answer: fiction. A corporation is a legal fiction ,  it exists only because enough people believe it does, and behave accordingly. So does a nation, a religion, a currency, and a human right. These are all what Harari calls "inter-subjective realities" ,  things that exist not in the physical world but in the shared space of human minds. They are like a collective dream that becomes real because everyone agrees to dream it together. The dollar bill in your pocket is paper. Its value is entirely a story that everyone on Earth is telling simultaneously. This is the key to human civilization: our unique ability to believe in, and organize around, shared myths. Not myths in the pejorative sense ,  not lies ,  but shared conceptual frameworks powerful enough to coordinate the behavior of millions of strangers who have never met. The rule of law is a myth of this kind. Human equality is a myth of this kind. Money is a myth of this kind. Without myths, we are clever great apes with good thumbs. With them, we built the Sistine Chapel, landed on the Moon, and wrote the Universal Declaration of Human Rights. Why Some Civilizations Dominated Others For centuries, the conventional explanation for why European powers colonized most of the world was essentially flattering to Europeans: they were smarter, more innovative, more culturally advanced. This explanation is wrong. The evidence does not support it. The actual explanation, which Jared Diamond spent a career documenting, is geographic. Eurasia's major axis runs east-west. The Americas' and Africa's run north-south. This single fact ,  the orientation of a continent ,  explains an extraordinary amount. Domesticable plants and animals spread easily along lines of latitude (similar climate, similar day length) but slowly across lines of longitude. Eurasia had more domesticable species to begin with, and they spread faster. Agriculture arrived earlier. Dense populations followed. Dense populations create the conditions for epidemic disease ,  and for immunity to it. When the Spanish arrived in the Americas, they brought guns, steel, and horses. But their most devastating weapon was invisible: smallpox, to which they had centuries of partial immunity and against which the indigenous population had none. The conquest was less a battle than a biological accident. It was geography, not genius, that gave Europe its head start ,  and the head start compounded over centuries into what looked, from the outside, like destiny. History's deepest lesson is the lesson of contingency. Things did not have to be this way. The arc of events is not the inevitable unfolding of some hidden logic ,  it is an accumulation of accidents, misunderstandings, and individual choices operating within structural constraints. The First World War, which killed twenty million people and set in motion nearly every major disaster of the twentieth century, began in large part because of miscommunication, the rigidity of mobilization schedules, and the particular cascade of decisions made by particular men in a particular week in August 1914. The Patterns That Do Repeat And yet history does teach lessons ,  not laws, but tendencies. Will and Ariel Durant, who spent fifty years writing an eleven-volume history of civilization, distilled their conclusions into one astonishing hundred-page book. Their findings are worth pausing on. Inequality is natural and persistent. In every society in every era, wealth concentrates. Not because of moral failure, but because of the compounding nature of advantage ,  those with capital can acquire more capital, and the gap widens until it becomes socially intolerable and either reform or revolution occurs. This cycle has repeated itself reliably for five thousand years. Civilization is not the default state. It is a hard-won and fragile achievement that requires constant maintenance. The institutions that sustain it ,  rule of law, free press, separation of powers ,  are not self-sustaining. They require people who believe in them, who staff them, and who are willing to defend them against the concentrated power of those who benefit from dismantling them. Rome fell not in a day, but over centuries, as each of its protective institutions weakened slightly, making the next weakening slightly more likely. History is not a story of progress interrupted by setbacks. It is a story of human nature ,  constant, predictable ,  operating in changing circumstances. Will & Ariel Durant, The Lessons of History Perhaps the most important pattern: power corrupts, not because powerful people are uniquely evil, but because power systematically insulates people from the consequences of their decisions. Robert Moses, the master builder who reshaped New York City for forty years, began his career as a genuine public servant and ended it as a man who demolished thriving Black neighborhoods to build parkways he explicitly designed to be too low for buses, so that poor people couldn't reach the beaches he built. He never changed his view of himself. He always believed he was doing what was best. Power had simply made him incapable of hearing the evidence to the contrary.
