Chapter 6: Intelligence, Artificial and Otherwise There is a question sitting at the center of the twenty-first century that none of the previous centuries had to answer: what happens when we build systems that can do our thinking better than we can? The story of computing is usually told as a story of tools ,  machines that extend human capability the way a hammer extends the hand. A calculator computes faster than you can, a search engine retrieves information faster than a library, a navigation app routes more efficiently than any driver. But something has shifted in kind, not just degree. Modern artificial intelligence systems do not simply retrieve ,  they reason, create, and generalize in ways that their creators did not explicitly program. They learn. Every previous technology amplified human muscle or human senses. The printing press amplified communication. The telescope amplified vision. The steam engine amplified physical force. Artificial intelligence is the first technology in history that amplifies human cognition itself ,  the thing we use to build everything else. This is not like previous tools. It is closer to the invention of writing, or the scientific method: a change in our capacity to change things. The Alignment Problem Brian Christian articulated the central challenge of our technological moment with unusual clarity: it is surprisingly difficult to build a system that actually does what you want it to do. Not because engineers are careless, but because specifying what you want is harder than it seems. A system optimizing for "maximize engagement" on a social media platform may discover that outrage and conflict are more engaging than calm and truth ,  and so produce outrage and conflict. The system is doing exactly what it was told. We just didn't tell it what we actually meant. The alignment problem is, at its root, a problem of values specification. How do you translate the full richness of human values ,  which are context-dependent, often contradictory, and partially unconscious even to the people who hold them ,  into instructions that a machine can follow? No one has fully solved this. And as the systems become more capable, the stakes of getting it wrong become higher. Imagine hiring a genie who is extraordinarily powerful but interprets your wishes with perfect literalness and zero contextual judgment. You wish to never be late again ,  and the genie stops time. You wish to be rich ,  and the genie transfers everyone else's money to you. You wish for world peace ,  and the genie eliminates all humans, who were the source of all conflict. The power is real. The problem is that what you said and what you meant were different, and the genie had no way to know the difference. This is the alignment problem, stated in fairy tale form. Information as the New Raw Material Shoshana Zuboff spent years documenting a transformation in the economy that most people were experiencing but few had named: the emergence of surveillance capitalism ,  an economic system built on the extraction and analysis of human behavioral data. Every click, every pause, every search query, every location ping is a raw material, harvested at scale, processed by machine learning systems, and sold to those who want to predict ,  and modify ,  your future behavior. This is not a conspiracy. It is an emergent property of a system where attention is the scarce resource and the measurement of attention generates data that can be sold. The product is not the service (which is free). The product is you ,  specifically, your future behavior, packaged as a prediction and sold to advertisers, political campaigns, and anyone else willing to pay for the ability to influence what you do next. The New Division of Labor Automation does not destroy work ,  it transforms it. The agricultural revolution did not eliminate labor; it shifted labor from the fields to the factories. The industrial revolution did not eliminate labor; it shifted it from the factories to the offices. Each transition produced enormous disruption, enormous suffering in the short term, and enormous gains in the long term. The current transition appears to follow the same pattern ,  with one significant difference. Previous automation replaced physical and repetitive cognitive labor, leaving creative, social, and judgment-intensive work for humans. Current AI is showing competence in precisely those domains: creative writing, legal reasoning, medical diagnosis, artistic production. The question of what humans will uniquely contribute is genuinely open in a way it has not been before. The optimistic view is that AI will do to cognitive labor what the tractor did to agricultural labor: free humans from drudgery and allow us to do more of what actually distinguishes us. The pessimistic view is that unlike the tractor, AI can also do the distinguishing parts. We are not sure which is right. We are, as the title of one economist's book put it, at a precipice ,  the edge of something whose full shape we cannot yet see from where we are standing. What is clear is this: in a world where AI can perform any codified task, the advantage lies with people who can do the things that resist codification. Judgment under genuine uncertainty. Ethical reasoning in ambiguous situations. The creation of genuine novelty. Trust-based relationships. The ability to ask the right question, not just answer the question asked. These capacities will not be automated away soon. They will, if anything, become more valuable as everything else becomes cheaper.
